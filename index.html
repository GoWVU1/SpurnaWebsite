The name & the myth

In 44 BCE, a Roman seer named Spurinna read the signs and warned Caesar of the danger gathered around the Ides of March. Caesar heard the warning. He simply chose not to listen.

A warning is only as powerful as the public's ability to see it — and act before the knife.

That is the gap Spurna was built to close. The ancient oracle read the flight of birds and the lay of the stars. We read the modern record: the votes, the statements, the promises made on the campaign trail and what came after. We take the oracle out of the temple and hand a copy to every citizen.

The name carries two truths at once: Spurinna, the one who reads the signs and sounds the warning, and to spurn, the refusal to passively accept whatever power claims. Reverence for the truth; irreverence toward the spin.
